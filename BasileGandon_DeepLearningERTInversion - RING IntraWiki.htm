Information

Level: 3A

Year: 2024 - 2025

Author: Basile Gandon

Supervisor: Paul Cupillard

Goal:

EN - Electric Resistivity Tomography (ERT) is a geophysical technique based on solving an ill-posed inverse problem in the Hadamard sense. On one hand, deterministic methods, often used to address these problems, are computationally expensive and prone to local minima. On the other hand, stochastic approaches allow a balance between exploring the solution space and computational cost. Among these approaches, deep learning appears particularly promising for solving inverse problems. Indeed, the universal approximation theorem, demonstrated by Leshno et al. (1993), establishes that well-designed deep neural networks can approximate any continuous function. Moreover, in the context of deep learning, the inversion cost is primarily front-loaded during the generation of training data and model learning, making the inversion itself fast and inexpensive once the network is trained. Although some studies have shown encouraging results compared to traditional methods, these models remain specific to a given measurement configuration. However, measurement conditions in ERT can vary significantly, both in methodology and in study dimensions. Our goal is therefore to develop deep neural networks capable of adapting to a wide range of ERT inversion configurations.

FR - La Tomographie par Résistivité Électrique (Electric Resistivity Tomography - ERT) est une technique géophysique reposant sur la résolution d'un problème inverse mal posé au sens de Hadamard. D'une part, les méthodes déterministes, souvent employées pour résoudre ces problèmes, présentent un coût computationnel élevé et sont sensibles aux minima locaux. D'autre part, les approches stochastiques permettent de trouver un compromis entre l'exploration de l'espace des solutions et le coût de calcul. Parmi ces approches, l'apprentissage profond apparaît comme particulièrement prometteur pour la résolution de problèmes inverses. En effet, le théorème d'approximation universelle, démontré par Leshno et al. (1993), établit que des réseaux de neurones profonds bien conçus peuvent approximer toute fonction continue. De plus, dans le cadre de l'apprentissage profond, le coût de l'inversion est essentiellement reporté en amont, lors de la génération des données d'entraînement et de l'apprentissage du modèle, rendant l'inversion rapide et peu coûteuse une fois le réseau entraîné. Bien que certaines études aient produit des résultats encourageants par rapport aux méthodes classiques, ces modèles restent spécifiques à une configuration de mesure donnée. Or, les conditions de mesure en ERT peuvent varier considérablement, tant dans la méthodologie que dans les dimensions des études. Notre objectif est donc de développer des réseaux de neurones profonds capables de s'adapter à une large gamme de configurations d'inversion ERT.

Preliminary work

A simple model was developed using a naive geological model and a basic neural network. The implementation runs on Google Colab (Drive access). The geological model is generated randomly, producing resistivity maps and apparent resistivity data computed with PyGIMLI. Below are examples of the generated data:

It also has some failures.

Left: Resistivity model output by the model. Right: Resistivity model corresponding to the input apparent resisitivity pseudo-section that was fed in the model. It seems that during this training, the model overfitted the data, or mode collapsed: the model always outputs the same result no matter the input. These initial experiments, although simplistic, suggest that the proposed approach warrants deeper investigation and refinement to address such limitations.

Bibliography

Following the preliminary experiments, a bibliographic review was conducted. The full bibliographic report is available here: rapport_bibliographique.pdf.

Key Takeaways from the Bibliographic Review:

  • Unlike traditional deterministic and stochastic techniques, which iteratively converge toward the best model(s) for the given data, deep learning seeks to approximate the pseudo-inverse operator. This offers a shift in methodology and opens the way to use both approaches.
  • The ERT inversion problem is horizontally invariant but exhibits significant variations along the vertical axis. Consequently, if a convolutional neural network (CNN) is employed, it should avoid weight-sharing along the vertical dimension to better capture the specific nature of the problem.
  • ERT data is often affected by noise, incomplete measurements (e.g., discarded datapoints), and variations in survey contexts. Therefore, the neural network must be designed to be both robust to noise and adaptable to diverse contexts.
  • Existing studies using deep learning for ERT inversion demonstrate promising results, particularly with CNNs. However, these models are generally limited to processing a single image shape and resolution, reducing their applicability to varied real-world scenarios.

Toy model

Based on the literature review, a promising approach to address the challenges identified is the implementation of a Dynamic Filter Network. This architecture integrates two deep learning components:

  1. A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP):

    • Input: metadata such as survey length, number of electrodes, geological context, and survey type.
    • Output: convolutional kernels, which are subsequently used to adapt the weights of the convolutional layers dynamically.

  2. A Fully Convolutionnal Neural Network (FCNN):

    • Input: a pseudo-section.
    • Output: a resistivity model.

The Fully Convolutionnal Neural Network takes as input an apparent resistivity pseudo-section and is asked to output its resistivity model.

The MLP generates the weights for the FCNN, making the latter entirely dependent on the former. During training, backpropagation updates only the MLP weights through the chain rule, propagating gradients from the FCNN output back to the MLP. As a result, the FCNN weights are not explicitly trainable parameters but are dynamically generated for each input context by the MLP. This design enables:

  • Flexibility in input shapes: The FCNN can process inputs of varying dimensions.
  • Context-aware adaptability: The MLP tailors the FCNN’s response based on metadata, ensuring a context-specific solution.

For each data sample, the input of the Dynamic Filter Network is the data sample. More specifically, the measured data (apparent resisitivity pseudo-section) is sent to the FCNN and the metadata (length of the survey and number of electrodes) is sent to the MLP. The MLP outputs convolutionnal kernels (or filters) that are then used to process the input of the FCNN. The outputs is then a function of the data and the metadata. Backpropagation is possible all the way up to the MLP.

A preliminary toy model was developed to validate the architecture, using randomly generated images as the dataset. The dataset is divided into sub-groups, where all images in a sub-group share the same shape, allowing mini-batch processing. Across sub-groups, image shapes vary randomly. Key aspects of evaluation include two types of generalization:

  1. Sample Generalization: the network's ability to correctly process and predict for new images with the same shape as those in the training set sub-groups.
  2. Context Generalization: the network's ability to generalize and perform accurately on images with completely new shapes, outside the distribution of training sub-groups.

This dual evaluation ensures that the model not only learns patterns specific to known shapes but also adapts to unseen contexts, validating its robustness and flexibility.
